Case Study 01 · Revenue Growth Agent — Revenue Growth

Every Monday morning, account managers knew which clients to call — and why.

An AI agent that reads every account weekly and recommends the next opportunity, who to approach, and the evidence behind it — so growth no longer depends on whoever happened to notice.

Business context

The information needed to spot a growth opportunity already existed on every account — project updates, consultant comments, contract dates, stakeholder changes. It just wasn't anyone's job to watch all of it, on every account, every week.

The problem

Expansion opportunities rarely showed up as a customer asking for more. They showed up as a pattern — a client hiring for a capability they don't have, a competitor's contract nearing expiry, an incident in their sector, findings from our own work left unremediated. Account growth depended on whichever account manager happened to notice. Experienced ones spotted these patterns intuitively. Newer ones didn't, so cross-selling was inconsistent, renewals stayed reactive, and revenue growth was impossible to scale.

The fix

A lead-scoring model ranks accounts by probability. It can't say why now, who to approach, or which past project to reference in the pitch. So rather than scoring accounts, I built something that does the reasoning an experienced account manager would — and hands over the result.

Case Study 02 · Client Intelligence Agent — Account Management

Two years of client history, delivered as a four-minute briefing before every meeting.

When an engagement manager left, the account cooled — not because demand disappeared, but because everything they knew went with them. This keeps it.

Business context

Client relationships ran through whoever was in the room. Steering committee notes, workshop discussions and delivery observations lived in HubSpot, in delivery documents, and in people's heads — mostly the last one.

The problem

When a consultant, engagement manager or account manager moved on, the stakeholder context and proposal history went with them — and accounts visibly cooled. Delivery teams also routinely noticed real sales opportunities mid-engagement — an upcoming cloud migration, a recurring security gap, a compliance deadline — that never reached an account manager, because nothing was capturing them. Institutional knowledge, not sales capacity, had become the real constraint on growth.

The fix

HubSpot could log that a meeting happened. It couldn't tell you that a note from March and a proposal from January describe the same emerging opportunity. So rather than another CRM field to fill in, I built the thing an account manager actually wants at 8pm the night before a meeting.

Business context

Leadership made pricing and staffing decisions using information scattered across Slack, Outlook, Excel and Power BI. Bringing it together took days, so decisions were routinely made on incomplete or outdated information.

The problem

A dashboard can only tell you what has already gone wrong. Clients had been asking for the same service, repeatedly, in Slack and Outlook for months — but that demand only reached a report once it showed up in the margin numbers, by which point it had been costing the business money for weeks. The signal that mattered most was always the one nobody was reading.

Case Study 04 · Client Response Agent — Client Experience

An AI agent that answers a client's questions 90% faster.

A client asking "why is this invoice wrong?" isn't a filing problem — it's a trust problem. And it was taking half an hour to answer.

Business context

Three strategic accounts generated between 105 and 150 billing and scope questions a week — invoice queries, "how many days do we have left," scope clarifications. Every one needed an answer sourced from three different places.

The problem

Answering one question meant checking the prepaid-hours tracker, the signed contract, and prior email threads — and consultants had to wait for a PM to do it on their behalf. The writing was never the slow part. Finding and cross-checking the evidence was. On accounts where an estimated SGD 80,000 a month of ongoing work rested on the client trusting a fast, correct answer, that delay was a commercial risk, not an inconvenience.

Search systems

The fix

A shared FAQ can't answer a question this specific — every query depends on that client's contract, their prepaid balance, and what was agreed over email six months ago. The three sources also regularly disagree. So rather than automating the reply, I built something that does the investigation and shows its working:

The contract says
Retest days were optional under the signed SOW — pulled from SharePoint.
The tracker says
10 of 12.5 prepaid days used. Two and a half remain.
The email thread says
No retest was ever requested, so it was never billed.
Conflicts found
None — but if the three had disagreed, the draft would say so rather than pick one.
